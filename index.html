Doctoral Research

Air Traffic and Urban Growth: Evidence from Airline Networks

Job Market Paper Link to Download

Despite substantial spending in airport infrastructure, little empirical research has done to justify its effects on economic growth in recent years. This paper provides a new perspective and shows strong evidence on the positive effects on the growth between air traffic and local economics up to the year 2016.

To examine the causal effects of air traffic on local economic growth, I innovatively apply the K-means clustering unsupervised machine learning technique to effectively choose the hubs from thousands of airports in the US. I combined the hub list with the Bartik shift-share(1991) instrument. I calculate the air traffic growth of small airports using the growth of their connected hubs multiplied by the percentage of the air traffic linked to each hub.

A More Generalized Way - The Effect of Internal Migration on Local Labor Markets: using American Cities in 1980

Working Paper Link to Download

This paper investigates the causal effect of internal migration on the local labor market. From the results, net-migration increases the local full-time employment probability but not the earning. No crowding out phenomenon occurred for existing residents by the in-migrants. The results imply that instead of wage reduction, workers have to work longer under the same wage in response to internal migration.

To address the endogeneity concern that migrants may be attracted to cities with good economies, I construct in-migration and out-migration rates as instruments for the net-migration rate. I use the variation in the shift-share measures and extreme weather events to predict the migrant flows to and from U.S. cities. I estimate migrantsâ€™ settlement patterns by distance between the sending and the receiving areas. I define the in(out)-migration rate for an area as the sum of the products of out(in)-migration flows and the corresponding migrating probabilities to (from) this area.
